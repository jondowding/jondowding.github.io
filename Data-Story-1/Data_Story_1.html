Postponed Safety Measures Real Reason for Hoboken Train Crash

The federal government's postponement of enacting Positive Train Control, PTC, technology allowed for recent train accidents, such as in Hoboken and Philadelphia, to occur.

By Jon Dowding

The train crash that killed a woman in Hoboken, NJ at the end of September joins a growing list of preventable train accidents within recent years.

The National Transportation Safety Board, NTSB, just closed the investigation into the derailment of an Amtrak train in Philadelphia in 2015, citing human error as the main factor of the crash.

According to preliminary reports from the NTSB, both crashes could have been prevented with the installation of Positive Train Control, PTC, technology.

PTC technology comprises of a three-part wireless communication system between a locomotive, a wayside station, and a back office server of all rail-related information such as speed limits and track compositions. This technology looks to prevent train-to-train collisions, derailments caused by speeding, track switching errors and usage of a track under maintenance.

This technology is already installed on thousands of miles of rails across the country. Congress even passed a bill in 2008 mandating itâ€™s installation on all privately owned railroads by December 31, 2015.

But that did not necessarily happen.

Many of the most recent train accidents and derailments are all caused from faulty equipment or human error. Each human error issue could have been prevented with the installation of PCT technology.

In October 2015, Congress passed another bill extending the deadline for installation of PTC technology by another three years. This allowed more time for major rail companies, such as Amtrak and NJ Transit, to draft plans for when PTC technology will be fully implemented on their railways.

Many railways set 2018 as their target year for full implementation. Yet many still have not submitted a PTC Safety Plan to the federal government.
