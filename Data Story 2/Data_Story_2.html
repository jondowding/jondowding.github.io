Land of the Free, Home of the Incarcerated

The US has the largest prison population of than any other country on the planet, raising questions about why people are spending so much time prison.

By Jon Dowding

Did slavery end?

On paper, Abraham Lincoln took care of that issue. Yet history shows that the shackles of slavery transformed into a new system of de jure segregation in the form of Jim Crow laws at the end of the Civil War.

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 eliminated the discrimination enforced by Jim Crow laws finally creating a new American society that recognizes everyone as a human being deserving of basic rights.

But just like Jim Crow, segregation morphed and people began dusting off the shackles once again.

In the 21st century, Jim Crow found a new home in the American prison system during an era of mass incarceration; an era where roughly 2.3 million other people are forced to call it home.

The United States has the highest prison population as well as the largest population per capita in the world.

“Let’s look at the statistics. The United States is homed to 5 percent of the world’s population, but 25 percent of the world’s prisoners,” said President Obama in the Netflix documentary “13th”.

World Incarceration Rates per 100,000

Incarceration rates in the United States began steadily increasing at the end of the 1970s once politicians launched a “war on drugs.” According to the Bureau of Justice Statistics, crack cocaine drug offenders make up 54 percent of drug offenders in federal custody as of 2012 and African Americans are the most commmon demographic to possess crack cocaine.

During the Nixon and Reagan administrations, strict anti-drug legislations allowed for large policing efforts to be enacted across the country. Laws such as the Anti-Drug Abuse Act and the Comprehensive Crime Control Act created harsher punishments and mandatory minimum sentencing for drug offenders.

In 1980, 40,900 people sat in federal prison for drug offenses. In 2014, 488,400 people sat in prison for drug offenses.

Those who leave prison also leave many rights behind, marking them as second class citizens. Convicted felons typically lose the right to vote, travel abroad, bear arms, be employed in certain fields, serve on a jury, receive public housing or social benefits and parental benefits.

The loss of certain civil rights relates convicted felons to slaves before any legislation was made to help them.

President Obama, towards the end of his administration, brought a greater awareness to the issue of mass incarceration especially by being the first sitting president to visit a federal prison. But many African American men still make up the largest majority of people incarcerated.

Michelle Alexander says in her book “The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness” there are more African American men incarcerated, than were slaves before the Civil War.
